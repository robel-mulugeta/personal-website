On Open Source for Healthcare Admin

The cost of building software has fallen off a cliff. A physician who can describe a workflow can now, with the right tools, build it. The constraint was never that clinicians lacked ideas about their own work; they have more and better ideas than any software provider ever will. The constraint was that turning those ideas into working software required a team they couldn't hire.

What's missing now is the scaffolding. Vibe coding an intake form is easy; vibe coding something that touches PHI, writes to a chart, respects an audit trail, and speaks FHIR is not, yet. That gap is exactly where open source frameworks belong. HIPAA-aware building blocks: a FHIR-native data layer, auth and audit baked in, prior-auth and scheduling and billing primitives you assemble. Compliance stops being a moat and becomes a default. The hard, boring, regulated parts get solved once, in the open, and everyone builds on top.

The result is a different distribution of power. Instead of exorbitant contracts and an eighteen-month implementation, a practice spins up the internal admin tool it actually needs in an afternoon. The EMR stops being a cathedral you visit and becomes a substrate you build on. Workflows get authored by the people who live them.

This is how open source ate every other category of infrastructure, and healthcare is overdue for it. The providers know the work. Give them the frameworks, and they'll build the tools the incumbents never bothered to.
