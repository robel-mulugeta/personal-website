House of CARRds

Contracted ARR, or CARR, measures revenue that's supposedly already committed to exist tomorrow. In a market that's privately terrified of an AI bubble, a signed multi-year commitment looks like the antidote to hype. A healthy dose of certainty.

The speed at which CARR has become a high profile metric in tech should worry us all, founders and investors. A market that suddenly prizes "committed" revenue feels like a market with false equilibrium. It's gone looking for a story it can tell itself about why all the spending makes sense.

The core problem is simple, and you can see it almost everywhere. A contract isn't cash, and "contracted" revenue isn't the same as revenue that has actually shown up, stayed, and spun the block after a lap around the sun. Contracts are being asked to paper over a lot of risk: that the counterparty stays solvent, that the product gets delivered, that the customer keeps paying once the novelty wears off.

A two-year-old coding-agent company posts a near-$500 million "annualized run rate," some of it acquired inorganically (not bad) and with almost no renewal history to show the revenue actually recurs.

Healthcare is where I see this problem emerge in a frightening way. Healthcare is where CARR and reality drift furthest apart, because the lag between signing a deal and delivering on it isn't measured in months but often 3+ years. Sell software to provider groups and you run into a strange combination: willingness to sign, and almost no ability to go live quickly. Providers are financially squeezed and genuinely eager for AI that codes claims, drafts notes, chases denials, and navigates a patient on their care journey.

But as Mike Tyson likes to say, everybody has a plan until you get punched in the face. EHR integration, the security and compliance review, and the go-live scheduling across dozens of sites that each have their own IT backlog. Punches... all to the face. A deal signed this quarter might not bill a single dollar of real revenue for a year or more, and a meaningful share of those deals might not fully ramp at all because product is still catching up. Healthcare CARR is frequently mostly C and very little R.

Between the signature and the go-live, there are countless ways a healthcare deployment stalls. It loses its internal champion. It gets descoped in budget season. It waits behind an EHR upgrade, or fails a security review, or simply never reaches the top of anyone's priority list. When that happens, the "contracted" revenue turns out to have never been revenue at all. It was just an option the customer chose not to exercise.

Unfortunately, reality doesn't go away when it's ignored. So, it's hard for me not to think about what happens to capital markets when we fund businesses on an aspirational metric. Three things come to mind:

1/ CARR misprices a whole category at once. Underwrite CARR as if it were durable ARR and you end up paying seasoned-revenue multiples for contracted promises that still carry implementation risk, renewal risk, and counterparty risk. The mistake isn't confined to one deal. It gets applied to every company that has learned to lead with the metric, so an entire cohort gets marked to a number that hasn't happened yet.

2/ It misallocates the capital itself. Money flows to the teams that are best at booking commitments, not the teams that are best at delivering on them. The defensible work is the implementation: the integration, the workflow change, the last mile that the CARR number skips right over. Reward signing over shipping and you get more of the thing that's easy (stories) and less of the thing that's hard (economic value).

3/ It creates a duration mismatch that only a rising market can hide. The capital goes out now, and the revenue, if it shows up, shows up later. Sometimes years later, on the provider's timeline rather than yours. To bridge the distance between what's signed and what's collected, these companies lean on the next round, the next buyer of the story. As long as money is cheap and confident, the bridge holds. When financing tightens, and it always eventually tightens, the gap between contracted and realized revenue stops being a footnote and becomes a funding hole.

The market has quietly agreed to treat a promise as a fact. Whatever reckoning is to come is getting deferred to the next person funding the next round. Likely derived from an unfounded confidence that they, too, can dodge a punch to the face from Mike Tyson.

On Open Source for Healthcare Admin

The cost of building software has fallen off a cliff. A physician who can describe a workflow can now, with the right tools, build it. The constraint was never that clinicians lacked ideas about their own work; they have more and better ideas than any software provider ever will. The constraint was that turning those ideas into working software required a team they couldn't hire.

What's missing now is the scaffolding. Vibe coding an intake form is easy; vibe coding something that touches PHI, writes to a chart, respects an audit trail, and speaks FHIR is not, yet. That gap is exactly where open source frameworks belong. HIPAA-aware building blocks: a FHIR-native data layer, auth and audit baked in, prior-auth and scheduling and billing primitives you assemble. Compliance stops being a moat and becomes a default. The hard, boring, regulated parts get solved once, in the open, and everyone builds on top.

The result is a different distribution of power. Instead of exorbitant contracts and an eighteen-month implementation, a practice spins up the internal admin tool it actually needs in an afternoon. The EMR stops being a cathedral you visit and becomes a substrate you build on. Workflows get authored by the people who live them.

This is how open source ate every other category of infrastructure, and healthcare is overdue for it. The providers know the work. Give them the frameworks, and they'll build the tools the incumbents never bothered to.
